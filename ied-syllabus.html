Introduction to Engineering Design

A full-year, aerospace-focused engineering design course built around design thinking, technical sketching, CAD modeling, FabLab safety, manufacturing documentation, prototyping, testing, and design review.

Course Overview

Introduction to Engineering Design is a project-based engineering course where students learn how engineers define problems, develop ideas, create models, document solutions, build prototypes, test designs, and communicate results. This version of the course uses an aerospace engineering focus, so students will apply core engineering skills to aircraft, spacecraft, drones, rockets, payload systems, structures, assemblies, manufacturing, and mission-based design challenges.

Course theme: Students learn general engineering design skills through aerospace missions and real-world engineering practices.

Course Goals

Design Like an Engineer

Define problems, identify criteria and constraints, brainstorm, evaluate trade-offs, and improve solutions through iteration.

Communicate Technically

Create concept sketches, isometric sketches, multiview drawings, annotated drawings, technical documentation, and design reviews.

Model, Build, and Test

Use CAD, measurement tools, FabLab processes, prototypes, data, and testing to support engineering decisions.

Course Schedule

Daily Class Structure

Lessons are designed around a 45-minute class period with bellwork, a short mini-lesson, guided practice, student work time, and an exit ticket.

Project-Based Pacing

Some design reviews, FabLab workdays, and testing days may extend across multiple class periods depending on project progress and equipment availability.

Required Materials

Students should bring the following materials to class and keep them organized throughout the year.

1.5" Binder

Used to organize unit packets, rubrics, sketch paper, project planning pages, returned work, and design review materials.

Lined Paper

Used for notes, reflections, written responses, planning, research summaries, and engineering notebook entries.

Graph Paper

Used for sketches, data tables, layout work, technical drawing practice, calculations, and prototype planning.

Pencils and Eraser

Pencil is required for sketching and technical work because engineering design requires revision and improvement.

Ruler

Used for sketching, layout, dimensioning, measurement practice, and drawing organization.

Charged Device

Used for CAD, research, digital submissions, unit resources, and presentation work when required.

Specialized paper: Students will also use isometric sketch paper, orthographic graph paper, multiview drawing templates, design review templates, and testing/data templates during the course.

Course Units

Unit 0

Course Launch, Procedures & FabLab Foundations
Classroom routines, engineering notebooks, digital workflow, FabLab safety, PPE, cleanup, and certification expectations.

Unit 1

Design Process & Aerospace Missions
Mission objectives, stakeholders, criteria, constraints, research, brainstorming, decision matrices, and design review.

Unit 2

Technical Sketching for Aerospace Design
Concept sketches, isometric sketching, multiview drawings, line conventions, annotations, and dimensioning.

Unit 3

CAD Modeling for Aerospace Components
Fusion workflow, parametric modeling, constraints, CAD features, model inspection, and export/documentation.

Unit 4

Measurement, Drawings & Manufacturing Documentation
Measurement tools, tolerances, manufacturing notes, BOMs, revisions, and fabrication planning.

Unit 5

Materials, Structures & Prototype Testing
Materials, loads, strength-to-weight ratio, failure modes, test plans, data, and redesign.

Unit 6

Assemblies, Mechanisms & Aerospace Systems
Components, joints, mechanisms, exploded views, assembly instructions, BOMs, and system documentation.

Unit 7

Final Engineering Design Challenge
Capstone-style aerospace design project with portfolio, prototype/testing evidence, and final design review.

Major Course Deliverables

Mission Concept Portfolio

Students define an aerospace mission problem, compare solution concepts, justify a design direction, and present an early design review.

Aerospace Drawing Package

Students produce organized sketching and technical drawing work using appropriate concept, isometric, orthographic, and dimensioning formats.

CAD Component Project

Students create parametric CAD models for aerospace-related parts and document their design decisions.

Manufacturing Documentation Package

Students create measurement, tolerance, drawing, BOM, revision, and fabrication planning documents.

Structures Testing Report

Students test lightweight structures, collect data, analyze failure, and propose redesigns supported by evidence.

Final Design Challenge Portfolio

Students complete a full aerospace-focused design challenge and present their final engineering design review.

Engineering Notebook and Binder Expectations

Engineering Notebook

Your notebook should show your thinking: dates, titles, objectives, notes, sketches, calculations, data, design changes, failures, and reflections. Mistakes and redesigns are part of engineering and should be documented.

Course Binder

Your binder should organize unit packets, graph/lined paper, rubrics, project templates, sketch pages, returned work, and design review materials so you can find evidence of your progress.

FabLab Safety and Tool Use

The engineering classroom and FabLab are professional workspaces. Students may not use any tool or machine unless they have instructor permission, required safety training, required certification when applicable, and required PPE.

Permission

Instructor permission is required before using any FabLab tool, equipment, or material.

Certification

Some tools require certification before use, including 3D printers, laser cutter, drill press, and CNC/advanced tools.

Workspace Reset

Students are responsible for cleanup, tool return, scrap disposal, material storage, and safe workspace reset.

Unsafe behavior may result in loss of FabLab privileges. Safety is a course requirement, not an optional expectation.

Late Work, Revisions & Make-Up Work

Late Work

Assignments submitted late may receive a deduction according to classroom and school policy.

Revisions

Engineering work often improves through feedback. Classwork, packets, design documentation, and project checkpoints may be revised when allowed by the assignment.

Absences

If absent, check the course website, class agenda, and learning management system. Students are responsible for missed work and project progress.

Academic Integrity and AI Use

Students may research existing solutions, but they may not copy another person’s design, CAD model, writing, sketch, code, data, or project without permission and citation. You must be able to explain your sketches, calculations, CAD files, data, and design decisions.

AI Use Policy: AI tools may be used for brainstorming, research support, troubleshooting, revision support, and feedback. AI may not be used to generate complete final submissions, final CAD files, final designs, fabricated work, or final written responses that are submitted as your own work.

Course Philosophy

Success in this course requires persistence, collaboration, careful documentation, safe work habits, and a willingness to improve through testing, feedback, and iteration. The goal is not always to get the perfect answer on the first try. The goal is to use evidence, creativity, and engineering thinking to improve designs over time.
